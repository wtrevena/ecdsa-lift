My reviewer verdict would be:

## What the paper is really about

The paper’s best claim is **not** “ECDSA is safe from p-adic lifting attacks.” That is too broad and already substantially known from the lifting literature.

The strongest defensible claim is:

> In a specific family of section-lift-error statistics for ordinary curves with `gcd(n,p)=1`, an apparently growing Gowers/Fourier anomaly is entirely explained by the public identity
> `δ(k) + δ(n-k) = [n]τ(G)`,
> and a mechanism-resolved null protocol is a better way to audit automated cryptanalytic searches than generic permutation controls.

That is a meaningful contribution. The PDF already acknowledges that the inertness theorem is not new mathematics and that the experiments are diagnostic rather than cryptographic-scale security evaluations, which is the correct positioning.

## Major strengths

### 1. The paper has a coherent and valuable failure story

The narrative is unusually strong: an automated search found an apparently meaningful `U²` anomaly; a conventional value-permutation control seemed to validate it; later controls showed the control did not separate the relevant mechanisms. That is a good publishable lesson for computational cryptanalysis, especially AI-assisted cryptanalysis.

### 2. The mechanism-derived controls are the strongest part

The structure of the controls is persuasive:

* increment-shuffle null to test the prefix-sum/walk artifact;
* half-sequence null to remove internal reflection constraints;
* reflection-only positive control to reproduce the anomaly;
* asymmetric-section converse control to destroy the identity;
* odd-section sham control to preserve it.

That is exactly the right experimental logic. The Phase 43 JSON matches the table-level story: increment shuffles remain near baseline, the half-sequence signal collapses, and synthetic reflection-only sequences track the real elevation across the clean curves. ([GitHub][1])

### 3. The paper is honest about scope

The manuscript explicitly states that it does not address nonce leakage, side channels, invalid/twist/small-subgroup attacks, MOV in general, quantum algorithms, extension-field cases, weakened curves, or non-section global lifts. That boundedness is important and should remain. 

### 4. The reproducibility ambition is good

The repository has `src/`, `experiments/`, `results/`, `paper/`, and a `run_all.sh` script, and the README says the experiments are intended to write JSON outputs under `results/`. That is the right artifact structure for this kind of paper. ([GitHub][2])

## Major issues to fix before submission

### 1. Proposition 2 appears to be missing a conjugation

This is the most important mathematical issue I saw.

The paper states the reflection-only constraint as something like:

`f(L+1-k) = β f(k)`

But the actual digit-phase encoding induced by `s(n-k) = C - s(k)` should give:

`f(n-k) = β \overline{f(k)}`

because

`exp(2πi(C - s)/p) = exp(2πiC/p) · exp(-2πis/p)`.

This distinction matters. If `f(k)` is uniform on the unit circle, the non-conjugated constraint `f(rev k)=β f(k)` does **not** create the same pseudo-variance mechanism as the conjugated reflection. The improper-complex-Gaussian argument should be written for the conjugate reflection, with the corresponding Fourier relation stated correctly.

This is fixable, but a mathematically alert reviewer will catch it. I would revise Proposition 2 and the surrounding text before submission.

### 2. The Dirichlet/variance argument needs either a proof or weaker wording

The paper’s `0.427√L` constant is central. The derivation relies on the periodogram behaving like a Dirichlet vector and uses that to get `Var(M) ~ 4/L³` rather than `20/L³`. The Phase 61 JSON supports the numerical claim: the Monte Carlo ratios track `4/L³`, mass ratios are around `1.5`, and `z/√L` stays close to the target constant. ([GitHub][3])

But the paper should distinguish:

* what is exact,
* what is asymptotic,
* what is a Gaussian/Dirichlet approximation,
* what is verified numerically.

Right now the proof reads more exact than it probably is. Either add a formal finite-`L` proof or state the Dirichlet step as an asymptotic approximation with an explicit error term or empirical confirmation.

### 3. The inertness theorem is basically right, but the wording around “public data does not determine” is dangerous

The theorem says the public data fixes `k mod n` but not `k mod p^{e-1}` over the natural lift. The later remark correctly notes that once a canonical representative `k ∈ [1,n-1]` is chosen, `k mod p^{e-1}` is determined. So the real claim is computational / section-invariance-based, not literal information-theoretic non-determination.

I would rewrite this part to avoid reviewer pushback:

> The public instance does not provide an efficiently computable, section-invariant value of `[k]c`; any computation of it requires the discrete-log representative or an equivalent oracle.

That is cleaner and aligns with the paper’s own “Consequence under ECDLP-hardness” framing. 

### 4. The Phase 60 converse control is slightly overclaimed

The PDF says breaking oddness destroys the identity and elevation together. But the raw Phase 60 results include a `genuine_half_binary` non-odd offset where antisymmetry is not single-valued, yet substantial `U²` elevation remains, especially at large `p`. For `p=10477`, the `genuine_half_binary` section still shows large z-scores, even though `antisymmetry_single_valued` is false. ([GitHub][4])

That does not necessarily invalidate the main result, because the paper’s preferred non-odd offsets `x(P)` and `x(P)+7y(P)` do collapse to baseline. But it does mean the general sentence “breaking oddness destroys identity and elevation together” is too broad.

A safer statement would be:

> Non-odd kernel offsets that destroy the low-rank reflection constraint collapse the elevation; odd offsets preserve it. Some non-odd offsets can leave residual low-complexity constraints and must be analyzed separately.

Also, Phase 60 appears to cover four curves in the JSON, not all seven Table 2 curves. The manuscript should not say “same curves” unless all seven were actually run. ([GitHub][4])

### 5. The repository/paper versioning needs cleanup

There are several reproducibility-paper mismatches that would annoy reviewers:

* The README still describes “Phases 40–42 and the paper” as the basis of a short 11-page PDF, while the attached v4 is 18 pages and relies on Phases 60–62. ([GitHub][5])
* The repository contains both `paper/ecdsa_lift_paper.tex` with “v3” metadata and `paper/ecdsa_lift_paper_v4.tex` with v4 metadata. A reviewer may compile the wrong file. ([GitHub][6])
* I did not see an obvious `requirements.txt`, `pyproject.toml`, `environment.yml`, `LICENSE`, `CITATION.cff`, or DOI archive in the repository file list shown by GitHub. The paper says an archival DOI will accompany the camera-ready, but for review you want this already settled or clearly marked as pending. ([GitHub][2])

Before submission, make the repo boringly clean: one canonical paper source, one PDF, one frozen release, one environment file, one license, one citation file, one Zenodo archive.

### 6. “Pre-registered” is too strong unless there is an external timestamp

The paper says the protocol was pre-registered before the clean rerun. If this was only internally fixed in the repo, call it a **fixed analysis plan** or **pre-specified rerun protocol**, unless there is a timestamped external registration or immutable commit demonstrating the timeline.

### 7. The paper still sounds too much like a cryptanalytic security claim in places

The abstract and introduction do a decent job limiting scope, but terms like “ECDSA regime,” “large-prime-order,” and “terminates one specific attack schema” need careful handling. `p=10477` is useful as a confirmatory computational curve, but it is not cryptographic-scale. The theorem may generalize, but the experiments do not.

I would consistently say:

> ordinary prime-order curves with `gcd(n,p)=1`, including the structural setting of ECDSA-type groups,

rather than implying empirical validation for deployed ECDSA curves.

## Venue recommendation

### Best-fit framing

The paper should be submitted as:

> **A reproducible methodology paper on false positives in automated computational cryptanalysis, illustrated by p-adic ECDLP lift-error statistics.**

Not as:

> “New theorem proving p-adic ECDSA lifting attacks fail.”

### Likely venues

**Best conventional journal fit:** *Applicable Algebra in Engineering, Communication and Computing* or *Designs, Codes and Cryptography*, but only after strengthening the mathematical proof and making the novelty clearly methodological. AAECC explicitly includes finite fields, algebraic curves, number theory, cryptography, algorithms, and computer algebra in its scope. ([Springer][7]) Designs, Codes and Cryptography publishes original research in design theory, coding theory, and cryptography, especially algebraic/geometric work, but it may demand more mathematical novelty than the current paper claims. ([Springer][8])

**Possible applied/security fit:** *Journal of Cryptographic Engineering* if framed around tools, techniques, implementations, and methodology for cryptographic analysis. Its official scope includes architectures, algorithms, techniques, tools, implementations, and applications in cryptographic engineering. ([Springer][9])

**Less ideal:** ACM TOPS. TOPS is broad security/privacy, but the current manuscript may look too narrow and not system/practice-oriented enough unless the AI-assisted automated-analysis methodology is heavily foregrounded. ([ACM Digital Library][10])

**Not appropriate in current form:** JOSS. The repo may have research value, but JOSS is for research software packages, requires OSI-open-source software and browseable hosted source, and reviews the software package more than the scientific paper. The current repo looks like an experiment archive rather than a polished reusable software package. ([Journal of Open Source Software][11])

## What I would require before acceptance

### Required revisions

1. **Correct the reflection-phase mathematics**: use `β \overline{f(k)}` where appropriate and rederive the Fourier/pseudo-variance relation accordingly.

2. **Formalize or soften Proposition 2**: either prove the Dirichlet/variance result rigorously or state it as an asymptotic model confirmed by Phase 61.

3. **Tighten Theorem 1(c)**: replace “not determined by public data” with a section-invariance/computability statement.

4. **Qualify the converse-control claim**: explain the `half_binary` Phase 60 behavior or remove broad language implying every non-odd perturbation collapses the signal.

5. **Add an appendix table for Phase 50**: the paper says the pattern is CM-independent, but the main table is `j=0`. Put the `j=1728` and non-CM results in the paper or supplement.

6. **Clean the repo**: canonical v4 `.tex`, requirements/environment file, license, CITATION, DOI archive, and updated README.

7. **Fix small textual inconsistencies**: “47 phases” versus appendix through 62, “same curves” versus Phase 60 subset, `run_all.sh` formatting, Table 2 spacing, and any stale references to an 11-page short paper.

### Strongly recommended additions

1. **A precise attack-schema definition**: define what an adversary is assumed to compute from `(E,G,Q)`, and distinguish offline labeled-sequence analysis from a public-key attack.

2. **Pseudocode for each null model**: value permutation, increment shuffle, half-sequence, reflection-only synthetic, kernel-offset converse.

3. **A finite-alphabet baseline check**: compare continuous `S¹` random phases against uniform `p`th roots of unity to preempt objections about the null distribution.

4. **A short “reviewer-proof” proof map**: one paragraph saying exactly what is known prior art, exactly what is new, and exactly what is empirical.

---

## Significance assessment

As cryptanalysis, the result is **negative and narrow**. It does not threaten ECDSA, and it does not prove all p-adic lifting ideas are dead.

As methodology, it is **interesting and publishable** if tightened. The strongest transferable lesson is that generic permutation controls can certify the wrong thing when algebraic identities impose ordered structure. That lesson is valuable beyond this specific p-adic ECDLP setting.

As mathematics, the inertness theorem is mostly a repackaging of known lifting/cohomological obstruction ideas. The pseudo-variance analysis is the more novel mathematical component, but it needs the conjugation correction and a more rigorous asymptotic treatment.

---

## My practical recommendation

Revise before submission. The current version is close enough that a targeted revision could make it credible, but I would not send it to reviewers until the Proposition 2/conjugation issue, Phase 60 overclaim, and repository versioning issues are fixed.

After revision, submit it with a title and abstract that emphasize **false-positive control methodology** over “ECDSA cryptanalysis.” The paper’s most publishable identity is not “we tried to break ECDSA and failed”; it is “we show exactly how a plausible cryptanalytic false positive survived a standard control, and we give a reusable protocol that would have prevented it.”
