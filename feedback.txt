# 2. The biggest remaining issue: the asymmetric-section converse control may be mathematically invalid as written

This is the most important problem I see.

The paper says it replaces the x-Teichmüller section with an asymmetric section τ′ that chooses the Hensel y-root by a “negation-insensitive rule,” namely smaller integer representative, so that τ′(−P) ≠ −τ′(P). It then reports that the antisymmetry ceases to be single-valued and that the U² elevation collapses. 

That would be an excellent converse control **if τ′ is genuinely a section**.

But the manuscript then says:

> “With τ′ exactly half the lift errors leave the kernel of reduction…”

That sentence is a red flag. If τ′ is actually a section, then for every k,

[
\delta_{\tau'}(k)=[k]\tau'(G)-\tau'(kG)
]

must reduce to

[
kG-kG=O
]

mod p. Therefore δτ′(k) must lie in the kernel of reduction for every k. If half the lift errors leave the kernel, then either:

1. τ′ is not actually a section,
2. the lift-error computation is not computing the group difference claimed,
3. the degeneracy filter is misclassifying kernel elements,
4. or the “smaller integer representative” rule is selecting a Hensel root that does not reduce to the original point P.

The fourth possibility seems especially likely. If you lift x and then choose the smaller of the two y-roots without enforcing the residue class y mod p, then for roughly half the points you have not lifted P, you have lifted −P. That is not a section of E(Fp) → E(Z/pe). It is a lift rule, but not a section.

This matters because the paper’s converse control is meant to show:

> Break τ(−P)=−τ(P) while preserving the same section-lift-error attack surface.

But if τ′ is not a section, the experiment instead shows:

> A non-section, partially wrong lift rule destroys the identity and the statistic.

That is much less probative. A hostile referee will attack this immediately.

## How to fix it

Construct a genuine asymmetric section by adding kernel offsets to a valid odd section:

[
\tau'(P)=\tau(P)+u(P),
]

where (u(P)\in \widehat E(p\mathbb Z/p^e)), but choose u so that

[
u(-P)\neq -u(P).
]

Then red(τ′(P)) = P for every P, so τ′ remains a true section, but τ′(−P)=−τ′(P) generally fails. Under this construction, δτ′(k) should always remain in the kernel. Then re-run the converse control. If the U² elevation still collapses, the converse test becomes very strong.

Until this is fixed, I would mark the converse-control claim as **not yet acceptable**.

# 3. Theorem 1 is improved, but Corollary 1 still overreaches slightly

The theorem section is much better than before. It now cleanly separates the algebraic CRT/well-definedness statement from the computational “no efficient algorithm known” remark. It also states explicitly that the computational part is state-of-knowledge rather than a theorem. 

However, **Corollary 1 still sounds too strong**:

> “For gcd(n,p)=1 no section lift error yields an efficiently computable function of (E,G,Q) that depends on the secret…”

That is not a corollary of the group-theoretic theorem alone. It depends on the standard computational hardness assumption. The theorem proves that the non-canonical lift error does not factor cleanly through the public instance without choosing secret-dependent representative information. It does not prove that no efficient algorithm can compute the relevant [k]c term.

The paper knows this and says so in the remark, but the word “Corollary” gives the statement more formal force than it earns. I would change it to one of:

**Option A: Rename it.**

> Consequence under the standard ECDLP-hardness assumption.

**Option B: Split it.**

Formal corollary:

[
\delta_\tau \text{ factors through } k\bmod n \iff c=0.
]

Computational remark:

> For c≠0, exploiting [k]c would require producing secret-dependent residue information not determined by the public instance; no efficient method is known short of solving the ECDLP.

This distinction matters because reviewers in cryptography are sensitive to exactly this boundary between algebraic non-canonicity and computational impossibility.

# 4. The paper’s core mathematical contribution is now much stronger

The new pseudo-variance treatment is a major improvement. Proposition 1 gives a clean identity:

[
E|Z|^4=2\sigma^4+|\rho|^2,
]

then uses pseudo-variance to classify U² mass inflation by index-coupling constraints. This turns the artifact into a general discriminator: no pseudo-variance gives the random baseline, maximal pseudo-variance gives the 3/2 reflection inflation. 

That is much better than the previous empirical/semi-empirical account. The closed-form 0.427√L growth law also gives the paper a genuinely quotable result. 

This is probably the paper’s most publishable technical artifact.

However, I would still make several refinements.

## A. “Exactly 3/2” should be phrased carefully

Proposition 2 says the reflection inflates the U² mass by exactly 3/2 and then gives asymptotic expressions with (o(1/L)). 

Those two phrasings are slightly in tension. For finite L and finite alphabets, there are usually lower-order corrections from the zero mode, self-paired modes, parity, and alphabet moments. The ratio is asymptotically 3/2 unless the model has been defined in a way that makes the exact finite-L ratio literal.

Suggested revision:

> The leading asymptotic inflation is exactly 3/2.

or

> The mass is (3/L+o(1/L)) versus (2/L+o(1/L)), hence the limiting inflation factor is exactly 3/2.

That is safer.

## B. Edge cases should be handled explicitly

The proof should explicitly say what happens to:

* the zero Fourier mode,
* the (L/2) self-reversing mode when L is even,
* the midpoint in the physical index domain when L is odd,
* finite μp-valued alphabets versus S¹-valued phases,
* whether the Fourier domain is (\mathbb Z/L) while the original sequence is indexed by (1,\ldots,n-1).

These are probably not fatal, but they are the kind of small mathematical edge cases that number-theory referees notice.

## C. The U³ claim needs either proof or softer wording

The paper now says U³ and higher sit at baseline because the reflection constraint affects only an (O(1/L)) fraction of shift-position pairs. That is plausible, but it is currently presented more like a derived result than a heuristic. If this is important, add a short lemma or appendix proof. If not, soften it:

> Heuristically, and confirmed numerically, the U³ excess decays like (1/L).

# 5. The carry model is useful but should be demoted slightly

The carry model is a nice addition because it addresses the earlier problem that the ideal reflection model does not exactly commute with base-p digit extraction. The paper now says digit 1 reflects exactly, while interior digits have borrow-induced deficits, with q2 and q3 around 0.2 on the clean set. 

This is good, but it should be presented as a **model**, not as a theorem unless fully proved.

Specific concerns:

1. The borrow probability qj likely depends on the constant C, the digit distribution, p, and the curve/sample. A fixed “≈0.2” should not be made to sound universal.
2. The formula ((2+(1-q_j)^2)/L) assumes failed-reflection pairs behave independently. That is plausible but should be stated as a modelling assumption.
3. The prediction “L ≳ 3000 should resolve digit 1 at 3.0 and digits 2,3 at 2.7” is useful, but reviewers may ask why the existing large row at L≈10638 does not already settle this in Table 1. If Phase 58 does settle it, summarize the actual result rather than only saying it is a prediction.

Suggested wording:

> A simple independence model for borrow failures predicts…

not:

> Modelling the rest as independent gives…

The latter sounds more definitive.

# 6. The statistical protocol is much improved, but still incomplete

The new §6 is substantially better. It now says z-values are standardized effect sizes against 300-draw empirical nulls, not calibrated Gaussian tail probabilities, and it includes look-elsewhere, equivalence, and half-sequence power checks. 

That directly addresses the previous critique.

But several improvements remain.

## A. The look-elsewhere analysis is too weak for the claim

The paper says the exploration spans roughly 47 phases × 3 digits × 8 curves × several encodings, that extreme-value theory gives an expected max |z|≈3.2–3.6, and that an empirical 10-statistic i.i.d. surrogate battery reached |z|=5.0. 

This does not fully support the abstract’s rhetorical claim that an apparent 5–11σ separation is “cheap.” A 5σ-looking effect may be cheap under a large, flexible exploration. An 11σ-looking effect is not explained by ordinary independent-test look-elsewhere arithmetic. The better explanation is that the baseline was wrong and a public algebraic identity generated a real non-random effect.

So the paper should distinguish two different mechanisms:

1. **Look-elsewhere false positives**: many probes make moderate z outliers likely.
2. **Wrong-null false positives**: a real public identity can create huge z against the wrong null.

The Gowers anomaly is mostly the second, not merely the first.

Suggested abstract revision:

> With enough encodings and transforms, apparently anomalous separations from a generic random baseline are cheap; they may arise either from multiple probing or, as here, from a real public structure tested against the wrong null.

## B. Equivalence testing needs more details

The paper says TOST excludes residual non-reflection H3 components larger than about 0.9 of the elevation at the smallest curve and about 0.25 at p=823. 

That is a good addition, but it needs enough detail to be auditable:

* What is the equivalence margin?
* Is the test on U² mass or U² norm?
* Is the residual defined additively in raw statistic, standardized z, or fraction of elevation?
* Are the 300 synthetic draws enough for the claimed interval?
* Are digits pooled or tested separately?

Right now the statement is useful but underspecified.

## C. The half-sequence power claim should be tied to actual numbers

The paper says an intrinsic H3 signal would appear at (z_{\text{full}}/\sqrt 2), and that the half test retained 4–9σ power on 6 of 7 curves. Good. But give a tiny table or parenthetical:

[
z_{\text{expected half}}, z_{\text{observed half}}
]

for each curve or at least min/max.

Otherwise the claim is plausible but not independently checkable from Table 1 alone.

# 16. Updated review decision

My updated referee recommendation would be:

**Major revision, leaning positive.**

The revision has materially strengthened the paper. The novelty is now correctly framed, the artifact mechanism is analytic rather than merely empirical, the scope is well bounded, and the anomalous boundary is clear. The paper now has a coherent publishable identity: a mechanism-resolved falsification protocol for automated cryptanalytic false positives, demonstrated on a p-adic ECDLP lifting anomaly.

Fix those two issues, add a few statistical/reproducibility details, and this becomes a strong submission for the right venue.
